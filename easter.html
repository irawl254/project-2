Easter Island

Easter Island, known as Rapa Nui by its native inhabitants, is a remote volcanic island in the southeastern Pacific Ocean. It is most famous for its nearly 1,000 massive stone statues called moai, which were carved by the Rapa Nui people between the 13th and 16th centuries. These statues, some towering over 30 feet and weighing several tons, are believed to represent ancestral figures and were placed on stone platforms called ahu.

The island was first settled by Polynesians around 1200 AD. Over time, deforestation, resource depletion, and internal conflicts contributed to societal decline. European explorers arrived in the 18th century, and the island was later annexed by Chile in 1888. Today, Easter Island is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, attracting tourists and researchers fascinated by its rich history and mysterious past.
